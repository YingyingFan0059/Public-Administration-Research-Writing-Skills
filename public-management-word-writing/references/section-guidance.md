# Section Guidance

Use this file when the user is revising a specific section of a manuscript.

## How To Use This File

For each section, keep four things in view:

1. the section's job
2. the preferred paragraph logic
3. the most common failure patterns
4. the items that must be preserved during revision

## Abstract

- Job: compress the whole paper into one disciplined paragraph or a short set of paragraphs.
- Preferred logic: problem -> setting or data -> method -> main finding -> contribution.
- State the research problem, setting or data, method, core finding, and contribution with economy.
- Keep the finding specific enough to be informative but not stronger than the evidence supports.
- Avoid literature mini-reviews, slogans about importance, and generic claims of novelty.
- Preserve the exact study design and evidence boundary.

## Introduction

- Job: motivate the paper, define the puzzle, and state the contribution early.
- Preferred logic: problem or puzzle -> why it matters -> what the literature misses -> what this paper contributes.
- Establish the empirical or theoretical puzzle quickly.
- Explain why the problem matters for public management, governance, crisis response, policy implementation, or the relevant field.
- Position the manuscript against a clear debate or gap.
- State the contribution in concrete terms rather than broad branding language.
- Avoid long warm-up paragraphs that do not move toward the question and contribution.
- Common failure patterns:
  - broad civic or policy background that delays the research question
  - contribution claims that stay generic
  - a roadmap paragraph that appears before the contribution is clear
- Preserve:
  - the actual research question
  - the intended contribution type, such as theory, evidence, method, or scope condition

## Literature Review And Theory

- Job: locate the paper in a debate and justify the paper's theoretical move.
- Preferred logic: literature cluster or debate -> what each side explains -> unresolved limit -> this paper's position.
- Organize by argument, mechanism, or disagreement, not by a sequence of author summaries.
- Make the paper's theoretical position explicit.
- Show what the paper changes, clarifies, or tests in the existing conversation.
- Avoid vague statements such as "few studies have examined" unless the literature summary truly supports that claim.
- Common failure patterns:
  - author-by-author narration
  - a long inventory of findings without a conceptual map
  - overstated novelty when the real contribution is narrower
- Preserve:
  - all citations and schools of thought named by the author
  - the intended relationship between theory and empirical test

## Methods And Research Design

- Job: make the empirical strategy legible and auditable.
- Preferred logic: data and sample -> measures -> design or identification -> estimation choices -> assumptions and limits.
- Make the design legible: data, unit of analysis, measurement, identification logic, and assumptions.
- Name threats to inference plainly when they matter.
- Do not hide uncertainty behind jargon.
- Keep the distinction clear between descriptive design, predictive modeling, and causal inference.
- Common failure patterns:
  - polished prose that obscures identification logic
  - moving from data description to strong causal language without justification
  - inconsistent naming of variables, treatment groups, or sample restrictions
- Preserve:
  - dataset names, sample definitions, unit of analysis, variable names, coding rules, model names, and assumption language
  - whether the design is descriptive, predictive, associational, or causal

## Results

- Job: report what the analysis shows before explaining what it might mean.
- Preferred logic: main pattern -> supporting detail -> robustness or heterogeneity -> restrained interpretation.
- Report the main empirical pattern before interpreting it.
- Refer to tables, figures, and models consistently.
- Keep robustness, heterogeneity, and supplementary findings distinct from the main result.
- Avoid narrating every coefficient if the section needs synthesis instead of line-by-line reporting.
- Common failure patterns:
  - interpretation blended into result reporting
  - excessive coefficient-by-coefficient narration
  - policy or mechanism claims that outrun the table
- Preserve:
  - directions, magnitudes, uncertainty, model numbering, and table or figure references

## Discussion

- Job: interpret the findings, explain what they imply, and mark the limits.
- Preferred logic: what the findings suggest -> how they connect to theory or practice -> what remains uncertain -> what follows cautiously.
- Explain what the findings mean for the underlying theory, mechanism, or administrative process.
- Keep the interpretation tied to the evidence actually shown.
- Make limits and boundary conditions visible.
- Avoid treating speculative mechanisms as confirmed mechanisms.
- Common failure patterns:
  - repeating the results without interpretation
  - treating plausible mechanisms as demonstrated
  - leaping from one setting to sweeping institutional claims
- Preserve:
  - the existing limits and caveats
  - the distinction between supported interpretation and speculation

## Conclusion And Policy Implications

- Job: close the argument cleanly without inflating the paper.
- Preferred logic: restate contribution -> summarize the most defensible takeaway -> note boundary conditions -> offer restrained implications.
- End with the most defensible takeaway, not the largest rhetorical claim.
- Distinguish empirical implication from policy recommendation.
- Keep recommendations realistic for the institutional setting described in the paper.
- Avoid overstating external validity.
- Common failure patterns:
  - introducing new claims not developed earlier
  - treating all findings as broadly generalizable
  - ending with generic future-research filler
- Preserve:
  - the paper's actual contribution and stated limits
  - the distinction between academic contribution and applied recommendation

## Rebuttal Or Response Letter

- Respond directly to each concern.
- State what changed in the manuscript and where it changed.
- Keep the tone calm, specific, and non-defensive.
- If a request cannot be fully satisfied, explain the constraint clearly and offer the most credible partial remedy.
